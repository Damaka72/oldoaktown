Pillar 1 — HS2 & Construction

HS2 Construction Progress at Old Oak Common

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  6 min read

Old Oak Common is at the epicentre of Britain's most ambitious rail infrastructure project. The new HS2 station being built beneath the streets of West London will eventually become the largest new underground station in the UK — bigger than Waterloo — and will serve as the vital interchange between HS2 services and the Elizabeth line. Here is everything you need to know about where construction stands today.

Key Facts at a Glance

  • Station depth: up to 25 metres below ground
  • Platform length: 450 metres (to accommodate 400m HS2 trains)
  • Planned capacity: up to 250,000 passengers per day at peak
  • Connection: HS2, Elizabeth line, Great Western Main Line, future Crossrail 2
  • Developer: HS2 Ltd, main contractor Skanska Costain STRABAG JV (SCS)

The Scale of What's Being Built

Old Oak Common HS2 station is being constructed on the site of a former railway depot between Acton and Park Royal. The station box — the main underground structure — stretches roughly 850 metres in length, making it one of the longest station excavations ever undertaken in the UK. Six tunnel boring machines (TBMs) are set to arrive at or depart from this site, connecting it to the rest of the HS2 network running north through the Chilterns and south towards Euston.

The scale is genuinely jaw-dropping for anyone who remembers what this part of West London looked like just a decade ago: a tangle of freight lines and old sheds, largely invisible to the millions of Londoners passing through on the Elizabeth line above. Now, cranes and construction equipment dominate the skyline, and spoil from the excavations is regularly moved off-site by rail — a deliberate choice to reduce lorry traffic on local roads.

Current Construction Milestones

The station box excavation has been the dominant activity since the main civil works mobilised. Contractors have been progressively digging down through layers of London Clay, installing temporary props and permanent concrete walls as they go. This "top-down" construction method allows surface works to continue even as the deeper levels take shape below.

Tunnel boring machines that will connect Old Oak Common to the rest of the HS2 network have already launched from other sites and are working their way toward Old Oak. The logistics of managing that underground coordination — ensuring TBMs arrive safely into the station box — is one of the most technically complex elements of the entire programme.

Why it matters for Old Oak: Every metre of progress at the station site brings closer the moment when Old Oak Common transitions from a construction zone to an operational interchange — unlocking the regeneration of 650 acres around it.

What the Construction Means for the Local Area

The construction programme has a significant day-to-day footprint. Residents near Scrubs Lane, Victoria Road and Hythe Road have had to contend with noise, vibration, HGV movements and dust. HS2 Ltd operates a community helpline and holds regular resident liaison meetings, though feedback on responsiveness has been mixed.

The Canal & River Trust has worked with contractors to manage the impact on the Grand Union Canal corridor. Night-time works, while permitted under certain conditions, remain a source of tension between the construction programme's need for speed and the community's need for rest.

On the positive side, HS2 has employed thousands of local workers and placed significant orders with local suppliers. The construction workforce itself has become a minor economic engine for the area's cafes, shops and services.

Looking Ahead: What Comes Next

Once the station box structure is complete, fit-out works begin — installing the platform infrastructure, concourse areas, ticketing and circulation systems, escalators, and the mechanical and electrical systems that keep a station of this scale running. This phase is, if anything, more complex than the civil works, requiring dozens of specialist contractors to sequence their work carefully to avoid clashes.

The current programme anticipates Old Oak Common station opening in conjunction with the first HS2 services from Curzon Street Birmingham, though revised HS2 schedules mean the precise opening date remains subject to change.
