Living in Old Oak Common: Your Complete Neighbourhood Guide

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  8 min read

Moving to Old Oak Common — or thinking about it? This is a neighbourhood unlike almost anywhere else in London. It sits at the intersection of two worlds: a working-class, multicultural West London community with deep roots, and an ambitious regeneration project that will reshape it over the next two decades. Living here right now means getting in early on something genuinely exciting — but it also means navigating the realities of a major construction zone. Here's everything you need to know.

Old Oak Common: The Basics

  • Location: Between Acton, Kensal Green and Park Royal, West London
  • Nearest stations: North Acton (Elizabeth line), Kensal Green (Overground), Acton Main Line (GWR)
  • Boroughs: Hammersmith & Fulham, Ealing, Brent
  • Population: Growing rapidly — tens of thousands of new residents expected by 2038
  • Character: Multicultural, working-class roots, increasingly mixed

What Old Oak is Like to Live in Right Now

Be honest with yourself: if you move to Old Oak Common in 2026, you are moving to an area that is mid-transformation. Parts of it are genuinely rough around the edges — HGV routes, construction hoardings, railway infrastructure and large areas of cleared land are the backdrop to daily life in some streets. But that picture is incomplete.

The residential streets around Acton Lane, Old Oak Lane and the roads between the Scrubs and the canal are established, quiet and often surprisingly pleasant. There are corner shops, community centres, places of worship, parks and schools that serve a population that has lived here for generations. The area's diversity — over 80 languages spoken in local schools — gives it a vitality that more homogeneous neighbourhoods can't replicate.

Getting Around

Transport is genuinely one of Old Oak's strongest suits. The Elizabeth line puts Paddington under ten minutes away and the West End within 20. Kensal Green Overground connects you to Clapham, Stratford and Watford. For Central London commuters, the journey times are competitive with far more expensive postcodes.

Local bus connectivity is more mixed. Some streets are well-served; others require a walk to a main road before you hit a reliable route. Cycling along the Grand Union Canal towpath is an underrated option for trips east towards Paddington Basin. Read our full transport guide for detail on every route.

Schools

The local school picture is varied, as it is across most of inner and outer West London. Several primaries and secondaries in the immediate area have strong Ofsted ratings and are popular with local families. As the OPDC's residential development delivers thousands of new homes, school place planning is a live issue — new primary provision is planned as part of several large developments, but there is concern in some quarters about whether supply will keep pace with the pace of new housebuilding.

Secondary provision is less concentrated locally, with many families using schools in Acton, Shepherd's Bush, Hammersmith and Ealing. The opening of new schools as part of the Old Oak regeneration is anticipated but not yet confirmed for the earliest phases.

Green Space

Old Oak Common is unusually well-provisioned for green space given its industrial character. Wormwood Scrubs — 180 acres of open grassland and woodland — sits on the northern edge of the OPDC area and is one of London's most underappreciated natural spaces. The Grand Union Canal towpath provides a green corridor for walking and cycling. Smaller pocket parks and green spaces are being incorporated into new residential developments as planning conditions.

Local secret: Wormwood Scrubs is genuinely spectacular for birdwatching in spring. The open grassland supports skylarks, kestrels and a host of migrants — remarkable given you're three miles from Marble Arch.

Shopping and Eating

The honest answer is that Old Oak is currently under-served for everyday retail and food. The main concentrations of shops are along Acton High Street (about a mile south) and in Harlesden and Kensal Green to the north. The regeneration will bring significant new retail, but it will be years before it materialises at scale.

What the area does have is a growing number of independent cafes and restaurants, particularly around North Acton and along the main bus routes — places that have followed the new residential development and that tend to have more character than the chain outlets that fill established high streets.

Nearest Supermarkets

Lidl on Scrubs Lane; Tesco and Sainsbury's in Acton and Kensal Green; Aldi in Harlesden

Leisure

Wormwood Scrubs open space; Powerleague Park Royal; Grand Union Canal towpath

Healthcare

Several GP surgeries on Scrubs Lane, Old Oak Lane and in North Acton; nearest A&E at Charing Cross or St Mary's Paddington

Libraries

Hammersmith, Acton and Harlesden libraries all within 1–2 miles; local community centres host reading groups

What's Coming

Patience is a virtue in Old Oak. The neighbourhood that will exist in 2035 — with a functioning HS2 station, tens of thousands of new residents, new high streets, schools, parks and cultural venues — will be substantially better-served than the one that exists today. Buying or renting now means accepting the current imperfections in exchange for being part of something from the beginning.
