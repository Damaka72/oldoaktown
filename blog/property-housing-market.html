Property & Housing Market: Buying and Renting in Old Oak Common

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  7 min read

Old Oak Common presents one of the most interesting — and genuinely complex — property investment questions in Greater London right now. The regeneration story is compelling: a major transport hub, 25,500 new homes, 65,000 new jobs, and billions of pounds of public and private investment. But the timeline is long, the risks are real, and the current market is still heavily influenced by the construction phase. Here's an honest assessment.

How the Old Oak Market Breaks Down

It's important to distinguish between different sub-markets within the broad "Old Oak Common" label, because they behave very differently.

North Acton: New-Build Tower Market

North Acton has seen more new residential development than anywhere else in the OPDC area over the past decade. A cluster of towers — mostly one and two-bedroom flats aimed at young professionals — has been built close to the Elizabeth line station. Prices here reflect the premium for Elizabeth line access and new-build specification, but the area still trades at a significant discount to comparable product in Paddington, Ealing or Canary Wharf.

The rental market in North Acton is strong. Young professionals working in Central London or at Heathrow value the connectivity, and rental yields on well-located new-builds are among the better in outer West London.

Established Streets: Victorian and Edwardian Stock

The residential streets between the Scrubs and the Great Western Main Line contain a mix of Victorian terraces, Edwardian semis and post-war housing. These are priced more modestly than comparable stock in Chiswick, Ealing or Shepherd's Bush, reflecting the industrial surroundings and construction activity. As the regeneration progresses and the neighbourhood improves, this discount should narrow — making these streets an interesting medium-term play for buyers who can tolerate the current environment.

New Development Launches

Several larger schemes are either on sale off-plan or about to launch. Buyers considering off-plan purchases should factor in the long lead times before completion, the uncertainty around the HS2 opening date, and the standard risks of buying into a neighbourhood that hasn't yet fully taken shape.

The regeneration premium: Property in areas undergoing major regeneration typically follows a pattern — prices rise ahead of the event (on anticipation), sometimes dip as construction disruption peaks, then rise again when the infrastructure opens. Old Oak is currently in the "construction disruption" phase. Buyers who can hold through to the HS2 opening are better positioned than those who might need to sell in the near term.

Affordable and Shared Ownership Routes

For buyers who can't access the open market at Old Oak prices, shared ownership schemes on new developments offer a route in. Several of the consented schemes have shared ownership allocations — typically 25–75% share purchases with rent on the remaining portion. Register interest with Homes England and the OPDC's affordable housing team early; demand consistently outstrips supply on these allocations.

Social rented homes — the most genuinely affordable tenure — are also being delivered as part of new schemes, though in proportions that community groups have repeatedly argued are too low relative to OPDC policy requirements. For detail on this, read our housing development updates.

The Investment Case

The honest investment case for Old Oak is: strong long-term thesis, significant short-to-medium-term uncertainty. The long-term case rests on:

  • Elizabeth line connectivity already in place and driving capital values
  • HS2 station (when it opens) adding a step-change in connectivity and prestige
  • Major public and private investment confirmed and actively underway
  • Current prices still at a meaningful discount to comparable West London locations
  • Long runway of population growth as new homes are delivered

The risks include: HS2 timeline uncertainty (covered in our delay analysis), potential for higher-than-expected new supply to weigh on capital growth, the construction environment depressing short-term quality of life, and the general macro risks facing the UK housing market.

For Renters

If you're renting rather than buying, Old Oak currently offers a genuine value proposition: Elizabeth line access at prices below equivalent locations further east or west along the line. The neighbourhood's rougher edges are manageable if you know the area, and the trajectory is positive. Renters moving in now are getting in ahead of the premium that will follow the HS2 opening.
