Transport & Elizabeth Line: Getting Around Old Oak Common

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  6 min read

Transport connectivity is the single biggest factor driving Old Oak Common's transformation from a forgotten industrial backwater to one of London's most strategically important development sites. The Elizabeth line already puts Old Oak within minutes of the West End and Heathrow; when HS2 arrives, it will add direct high-speed connections to Birmingham, Manchester and beyond. Here's how transport provision is evolving and what it means for residents and businesses.

Transport Connections: Current & Planned

  • Elizabeth line: North Acton station — 10 min to Paddington, 40 min to Heathrow T2/T3
  • Overground: Kensal Green — Watford Junction / Clapham Junction
  • Great Western Main Line: Acton Main Line station
  • HS2 (future): Old Oak Common interchange — Birmingham 45 min
  • Bus routes: 7, 70, 220, 228, 316, 487 serve the wider area
  • Cycling: Superhighway CS3 nearby; new routes planned in OPDC masterplan

The Elizabeth Line Effect

The opening of the Elizabeth line (Crossrail) in 2022 was transformative for West London. For Old Oak Common, the most relevant station is North Acton — technically not in the OPDC area but close enough to be the primary rail option for many residents and workers. Journey times to Paddington are under ten minutes, and onward connections to the City, Canary Wharf and Heathrow make North Acton one of the most well-connected stations in outer London.

The Elizabeth line effect on Old Oak is visible in the development activity clustering around North Acton. Developers are acutely aware that buyers and renters pay a premium for walk-to-Elizabeth-line access, and the pipeline of new residential towers in this part of the OPDC area reflects that commercial reality.

HS2: The Future Interchange

When the Old Oak Common HS2 station opens, it will be a step-change in connectivity of an order not seen in this part of London for a generation. The station will serve as the London terminus for HS2 Phase 1 until the extension to Euston is complete, meaning all HS2 services to Birmingham and beyond will call here. The interchange with the Elizabeth line will allow passengers to transfer between the two systems without surfacing — a genuine transport hub comparable to St Pancras/King's Cross in scale of interchange if not in historic grandeur.

For Old Oak residents and businesses, this means direct high-speed access to central Birmingham in around 45 minutes — a journey time that puts Old Oak, economically speaking, closer to Birmingham's business core than many parts of Greater Birmingham are to the city centre.

The commuter calculation: A professional working in Birmingham city centre and living in Old Oak Common could face a commute of under an hour door-to-door, once HS2 is operational. That will have significant implications for both property values and the character of the area.

Buses and Local Connectivity

For all the focus on rail, many Old Oak residents rely on buses for local trips — to the shops, schools, health centres and leisure facilities. The bus network in and around the OPDC area has been under pressure, with some routes affected by construction-related road closures. TfL and the OPDC have committed to reviewing bus provision as population grows, but residents have reported gaps in coverage, particularly in the southern parts of the development area around Old Oak Lane.

Cycling Infrastructure

The OPDC masterplan includes ambitious plans for a network of segregated cycle routes connecting Old Oak to the wider London cycling network. New residential schemes are required to provide cycle storage, and the OPDC is negotiating with TfL to extend Santander Cycle hire docking stations into the area. Progress has been incremental rather than transformational so far, but the direction of travel is positive.

The Grand Union Canal towpath provides a safe, car-free route connecting Old Oak to Paddington Basin in the east and beyond in the west — a genuine asset for cyclists and walkers that is underused relative to its potential.

What's Still Missing

The current transport picture has clear gaps. The area between the HS2 construction zone and the Elizabeth line stations is poorly served — pedestrian routes are often circuitous and uninviting, particularly at night. The new road network planned as part of the wider regeneration will help, but it is many years from delivery for the central parts of the development area.
