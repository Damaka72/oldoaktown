OPDC Planning Decisions: What's Being Approved in Old Oak Common

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  6 min read

The Old Oak and Park Royal Development Corporation (OPDC) is unlike any other planning authority in London. Established in 2015 as a Mayoral Development Corporation, it holds strategic planning powers for approximately 650 acres of land around the future HS2 and Elizabeth line interchange at Old Oak Common. Every major planning decision it makes shapes the character, density and social mix of a neighbourhood that will house tens of thousands of people.

OPDC at a Glance

  • Area covered: ~650 acres across three boroughs (Hammersmith & Fulham, Ealing, Brent)
  • Target homes: 25,500 new homes by 2038
  • Target jobs: 65,000 new jobs
  • Local Plan adopted: 2022
  • Planning committee meets monthly; agendas published on the GLA website

How OPDC Planning Works

The OPDC acts as the local planning authority for its area, meaning planning applications — from a single house extension to a 40-storey tower — are decided by the OPDC rather than by the three surrounding London boroughs. This unusual arrangement was designed to ensure a coherent, ambitious approach to the regeneration, free from the sometimes parochial concerns that can slow major schemes through conventional borough planning committees.

The OPDC's Local Plan, adopted in 2022, sets out the vision: a high-density, mixed-use neighbourhood with generous public realm, strong transport connections, and an emphasis on genuinely affordable housing. All planning decisions are measured against that plan's policies.

Major Planning Decisions to Watch

Several significant planning applications are either recently determined or progressing through the system. These fall into a few broad categories.

Station Quarter Masterplans

The land immediately surrounding the HS2 station — broadly known as North Acton, the station quarter and Scrubs Lane — is the subject of multiple masterplan applications from major developers including Canary Wharf Group, Stanhope and Volker Fitzpatrick. These are outline applications at this stage, establishing the quantum of development (number of homes, commercial floorspace, public realm) without yet fixing detailed design. Once outline consent is granted, reserved matters applications will follow covering the design of individual plots.

Affordable Housing Requirements

One of the most contentious issues in every major planning application is affordable housing. The OPDC's policy requires 35% affordable housing across a development, rising to 50% where public land is involved. In practice, many applications come in below policy, triggering negotiations between the OPDC and developers. Viability assessments — submitted by developers to justify a lower affordable housing provision on financial grounds — are a recurring flashpoint, with community groups often pushing for greater transparency.

Community tip: All OPDC planning committee reports, including viability assessments, are published on the GLA planning portal. Members of the public can speak at planning committee meetings — check the OPDC website for upcoming agendas and registration deadlines.

Industrial Land Designations

Park Royal — the southern portion of the OPDC area — is one of Europe's largest industrial estates. Its businesses, from food manufacturers to logistics operators, provide thousands of jobs for local people. The OPDC's Local Plan protects "Strategic Industrial Land" from residential conversion, but there is constant pressure from landowners and developers to reclassify sites. Each planning decision that touches the industrial land boundary matters enormously for the long-term employment character of the area.

Upcoming Decisions

The OPDC planning pipeline for the next 12–18 months includes several applications of note:

  • Reserved matters applications for early residential phases from developers with existing outline consent
  • New applications for student accommodation near the North Acton Elizabeth line station
  • Amendments to existing consents reflecting revised viability positions post-construction cost inflation
  • A potential review of the Old Oak North masterplan area following updated infrastructure cost assessments

How to Have Your Say

The OPDC encourages public participation in the planning process. Comments on live planning applications can be submitted via the GLA planning portal, and the OPDC holds regular consultation events when significant applications come forward. The Old Oak Neighbourhood Forum also holds its own meetings where planning matters are regularly discussed — a valuable community voice in a process that can feel remote and technical.
