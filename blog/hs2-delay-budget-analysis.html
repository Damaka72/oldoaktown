HS2 Delay & Budget Analysis: What the Cost Overruns Mean for Old Oak Common

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  9 min read

HS2 has become synonymous with cost overruns, political controversy and revised timelines. For those of us tracking Old Oak Common's transformation, the HS2 story isn't an abstract infrastructure debate — it's the central axis around which the entire regeneration turns. Every delay to HS2 is, in some sense, a delay to Old Oak. This analysis sets out what we know, what we don't, and what it means for the neighbourhood.

HS2 Budget: A History of Revision

  • Original 2010 budget estimate: ~£32bn
  • 2019 revised estimate: £72–98bn (2019 prices)
  • Oakervee Review (2020): confirmed cost pressures, recommended continuation
  • Phase 2 (Manchester) cancelled: October 2023
  • Current programme: Phase 1 (London–Birmingham) only, revised delivery schedule
  • Old Oak Common station: construction ongoing, opening date subject to programme review

How We Got Here

HS2 was conceived as a transformational infrastructure project — a new high-speed spine for England connecting London, the Midlands and the North. The original business case rested on three pillars: capacity relief on the existing West Coast Main Line, journey time savings, and regional economic rebalancing. At the time it was conceived, the price tag seemed large but manageable for a project of national significance.

What happened next is a case study in megaproject cost escalation. A combination of scope changes, optimism bias in the original estimates, ground conditions more challenging than anticipated, the complexity of building through urban areas, supply chain inflation, and — critically — the long gap between initial designs and actual construction all contributed to costs that escalated far beyond the original envelope.

The 2023 decision to cancel Phase 2 north of Birmingham — announced by then-Prime Minister Rishi Sunak at the Conservative Party conference — was a political earthquake. It saved significant sums on paper but came at the cost of the northern connectivity that had been the project's primary economic rationale. Critics argued, with considerable force, that a high-speed railway connecting London to Birmingham but not to Manchester or Leeds serves commuters far more than it rebalances the national economy.

What Phase 2 Cancellation Means for Old Oak

Old Oak Common sits on Phase 1, which remains live. The station here will be built and will eventually open. But the cancellation of Phase 2 changes the economic logic of the station in subtle but important ways.

The key shift: Old Oak Common was designed as a through station on a national high-speed network. Without Phase 2, it becomes primarily an interchange between HS2 (serving Birmingham) and the Elizabeth line — still enormously valuable, but no longer the gateway to the whole of the North of England.

For Old Oak residents and property buyers, the practical implication is that the "45 minutes to Birmingham" headline remains true. The "2 hours to Manchester" that was once part of the HS2 prospectus has been quietly dropped. Whether this materially affects Old Oak's attractiveness depends heavily on individual buyers' priorities — many will be commuting to Central London on the Elizabeth line and will care far more about that connection than about HS2 to the North.

The Opening Date Question

When will Old Oak Common HS2 station actually open? This is the question most residents and developers want answered, and the honest response is: we don't know with precision.

Original target: early 2030s

The initial programme anticipated Old Oak Common opening as HS2's London terminus ahead of the Euston extension.

Revised expectations: mid-to-late 2030s

Construction programme slippage and programme reviews have pushed the anticipated opening window rightward.

Euston extension: uncertain

The extension from Old Oak Common to Euston — Phase 1's southern terminus — has been the subject of repeated review. Its timing affects the overall programme significantly.

Knock-on Effects for Housing and Investment

Every year of delay to the HS2 station opening is, broadly speaking, a year of delay to the highest-value development phases around Old Oak North. Developers who have secured planning consent are waiting for the infrastructure to be in place before committing to the most ambitious phases of their schemes. The station is the catalyst; without it, the case for office and major retail development in the new town centre is significantly weaker.

Residential development in the parts of the OPDC area closest to existing Elizabeth line stations — particularly North Acton — is less dependent on HS2 and is proceeding largely on its own timetable. But the transformational vision for Old Oak as a genuinely new London neighbourhood, rather than an extension of the existing suburbs, is fundamentally tied to HS2.

The Case for Cautious Optimism

It would be wrong to read the HS2 story as pure bad news for Old Oak. The station is being built. The money has been spent. The tunnels are being bored. Whatever the political drama at Westminster, the physical reality on the ground at Old Oak Common is of a genuinely major infrastructure project in active construction.

The OPDC has been thoughtful about not tying its entire delivery strategy to HS2 opening dates, recognising that early sites can come forward independently. And the Elizabeth line — already open and already transformative — provides a solid connectivity base that benefits Old Oak regardless of when HS2 opens.

The long view is important here. Old Oak Common in 2026 is an incomplete picture. The neighbourhood that will exist in 2040 will be substantially different — and substantially better connected — regardless of exactly which year the HS2 trains start running.
