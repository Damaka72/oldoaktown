Jobs & Employment: 65,000 New Jobs Coming to Old Oak Common

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  7 min read

Old Oak Common and Park Royal together represent one of the most significant employment zones in Greater London — and one that is on the verge of a major transformation. Park Royal is already one of Europe's largest industrial estates, employing tens of thousands of people across manufacturing, logistics, food production and trades. As the HS2-led regeneration unfolds, the OPDC is targeting 65,000 new jobs in the area by the mid-2030s. Here's where those jobs are coming from, who they're for, and how to access them.

Employment: Key Numbers

  • Current jobs in the OPDC area: ~40,000 (predominantly Park Royal industrial)
  • Target new jobs by mid-2030s: 65,000
  • HS2 construction workforce: thousands of direct and indirect jobs, now through to station opening
  • Key sectors: construction, manufacturing, logistics, food production, professional services, retail, hospitality
  • Major employer: Park Royal Business Group represents 1,600+ businesses

Jobs Available Right Now

The biggest single source of local employment in 2026 is, straightforwardly, the HS2 construction programme itself. The station box excavation, tunnelling works and associated infrastructure have created thousands of direct and indirect jobs — from civil engineers and tunnelling operatives to logistics coordinators, health and safety managers and administrative staff. HS2 Ltd has a commitment to local labour and training programmes, meaning West London residents have a genuine route into construction careers through the programme.

Beyond HS2, Park Royal's established businesses continue to employ significant numbers. Food and drink manufacturing — including major national brands with large facilities in the estate — offers entry-level and skilled roles in production, quality control and logistics. The estate's logistics and distribution companies are also perennially hiring, particularly in last-mile delivery and warehousing operations.

Sectors Driving Future Job Growth

Professional & Office Services

The new Old Oak town centre will eventually attract office occupiers, particularly those benefiting from HS2 connectivity to Birmingham. Early-phase commercial development is likely to focus on flexible workspace and smaller occupiers.

Retail & Hospitality

A new residential population of 50,000+ will create demand for cafes, restaurants, shops and leisure facilities — the everyday service economy that a neighbourhood needs to function.

Construction & Trades

The pipeline of residential and commercial development will sustain construction employment well beyond the HS2 programme, with decades of housebuilding and fit-out work to follow.

Creative & Digital

Affordable workspace in former industrial buildings is attracting creative and tech businesses. As the neighbourhood matures and becomes better-connected, this cluster is expected to grow.

Skills and Training

One of the most important questions for local residents is whether the new jobs being created will actually be accessible to the communities already living in Old Oak and Park Royal. These are areas with significant pockets of deprivation, and there is a real risk that a wave of new professional-sector jobs benefits incoming workers rather than established residents.

The OPDC and HS2 Ltd have both made commitments to local employment and training. HS2's Skills Academy has delivered construction training to local people, creating pathways into trades and technical roles that pay well and offer long-term career prospects. The Mayor of London's skills programmes also operate in the area, with targeted provision for residents facing barriers to employment.

For job seekers: HS2 Ltd publishes live vacancies on their website, and the Supply Chain Sustainability School has information on construction career pathways. The Park Royal Business Group runs networking events and can connect local residents with area employers. West London College has campuses serving the area and offers vocational and professional qualifications aligned with local demand sectors.

The Industrial Land Question

One of the most consequential planning debates for employment in the area concerns the future of Park Royal's industrial land. The OPDC's Local Plan protects Strategic Industrial Land from residential conversion, reflecting the view that jobs in manufacturing and logistics are genuinely valuable and shouldn't be sacrificed to housing demand. But individual landowners — holding sites that have risen dramatically in value — apply constant pressure to release industrial land for residential development.

This matters because the jobs in Park Royal's industrial estate — many of them accessible without a university degree and paying decent wages — are exactly the kind of work that supports the economic diversity of West London. Lose the industrial base, and you lose a critical employment option for a significant part of the population.

Looking at the Long Term

The 65,000 new jobs target is ambitious and long-term. The most credible path to achieving it runs through a combination of: retaining and growing Park Royal's industrial employment base, developing new office and commercial space in the Old Oak town centre as the HS2 station opens, and supporting the growth of the service economy that will follow the residential population. None of this happens quickly — but the direction is clear.
