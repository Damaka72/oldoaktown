Local Business Spotlights: Old Oak Common & Park Royal

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  6 min read

Old Oak Common and Park Royal together form one of the most economically complex corners of West London. On one side: Europe's largest industrial estate, home to food manufacturers, logistics firms and creative industries that have operated here for generations. On the other: an emerging residential neighbourhood with a growing appetite for the cafes, shops and services that make a community feel like home. The businesses navigating this moment of transformation are doing something remarkable.

The Business Landscape

Park Royal Industrial Estate is home to around 1,600 businesses employing some 40,000 people — making it one of the largest concentrations of employment in Greater London. The range of activity is staggering: major food and drink brands including McVitie's and Warburtons have significant facilities here; logistics and distribution companies occupy large modern sheds; and a growing cluster of creative and digital businesses is finding affordable workspace in older industrial buildings.

The arrival of HS2 and the associated regeneration is already changing the economics of the area. Land values are rising, long-standing industrial tenancies are being reviewed, and some businesses are having to navigate the tension between staying rooted in a community they've been part of for decades and adapting to an area that is changing around them.

Businesses to Know

Food & Drink Manufacturing

Park Royal's Food Production Cluster

Park Royal is London's food production heartland. From bakeries supplying supermarkets across the UK to artisan food makers selling at farmers' markets, the industrial estate hosts an extraordinary range of food businesses. Several are exploring direct-to-consumer routes as the local residential population grows — watch this space for new local food brands to emerge.

Café & Hospitality

The New Wave of Local Cafes

A new generation of cafes and coffee shops is following the residential development northwards from Acton and Shepherd's Bush. These aren't chain outlets — they're independent operators who see Old Oak's emerging neighbourhood as an opportunity to build something before rents become prohibitive. Look out for new openings around North Acton and along the Scrubs Lane corridor.

Creative & Digital

Creative Industries in Former Industrial Space

Affordable, characterful industrial space has always attracted creative businesses. Architects, designers, filmmakers and tech startups are clustered in a number of Park Royal estates, drawn by large floorplates, good transport links and a gritty aesthetic that Shoreditch or Bermondsey prices have long since priced out. The OPDC's Local Plan tries to protect some of this workspace, but the pressure from residential conversion is constant.

Construction & Trades

Local Construction Supply Chain

With billions of pounds of construction activity underway in the immediate area, local builders, suppliers and trades businesses are among the main beneficiaries of the regeneration. Several Old Oak-based contractors have grown significantly on the back of HS2-related and residential construction work.

Challenges Facing Local Businesses

The regeneration creates winners and losers. Industrial businesses on land that has risen sharply in value face difficult decisions: sell up and relocate, negotiate new leases at higher rents, or find ways to adapt their business model for a changing neighbourhood. Many long-standing employers are quietly concerned that the regeneration will ultimately displace them, costing the area the very jobs and economic diversity that makes it special.

New businesses, meanwhile, face the challenge of serving a population that is still relatively small but growing fast — the classic chicken-and-egg problem of building a business in a neighbourhood that hasn't fully arrived yet.
