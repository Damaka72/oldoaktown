Housing Development Updates: 25,500 New Homes Coming to Old Oak Common

By Old Oak Town Editorial  |  June 2026  |  7 min read

Old Oak Common is set to become one of London's most significant new residential neighbourhoods. The 25,500 homes planned for the area will be delivered over a period stretching into the 2040s, with earlier phases already taking shape. This guide tracks which sites are active, what types of homes are being built, and what affordable housing residents can realistically expect.

Housing at a Glance

  • Total homes planned: 25,500 across the OPDC area
  • Affordable housing target: 35–50% depending on land type
  • Mix: studios to family-sized homes, social rent, shared ownership, private sale
  • Key delivery zones: Old Oak North, Old Oak South, Scrubs Lane, North Acton
  • Phasing: Early sites active now; bulk of delivery post-HS2 station opening

The Phasing Plan

Not all 25,500 homes will arrive at once — they will be delivered in phases tied to infrastructure milestones. The current OPDC delivery strategy distinguishes between "early sites" that can come forward on land already accessible and serviced, and "later sites" that depend on new roads, utilities and public realm being put in place as the HS2 station construction completes.

Early phases are concentrated around North Acton, which already benefits from Elizabeth line connectivity, and along the Scrubs Lane corridor where some remediation and site preparation work is well advanced. These areas are producing the first new homes — a mix of private sale and affordable tenures from developers including Helical, Vastint and Berkeley Group subsidiaries.

Affordable Housing: The Reality

The OPDC's policy ambition of 35–50% affordable housing is laudable, but the reality on the ground is more complicated. Construction cost inflation since 2022, rising interest rates, and the specific challenges of building on former industrial land — contamination, abnormal infrastructure costs — have put pressure on viability. Several applications have been approved with lower affordable housing proportions than policy requires, with "viability review mechanisms" that allow the proportion to be revisited as development proceeds and (developers hope) margins improve.

Community groups, including the Old Oak Neighbourhood Forum, have consistently pushed back on this. Their argument is simple: if affordable housing is deferred or reduced at the planning stage, it is extremely difficult to claw back later. Once a private block is built at a lower affordable proportion, it stays that way.

What this means for you: If you are hoping to buy a shared ownership home or apply for social rent in Old Oak, the current advice is to register your interest with the OPDC's affordable housing register and monitor individual development launches — competition is expected to be intense for the early phases.

Key Development Sites

Old Oak North

The largest single development area, covering land north of the Grand Union Canal. This is the long-term heart of the new neighbourhood, planned to deliver the highest concentration of homes and the main new town centre. It is also the area most dependent on the HS2 station infrastructure being in place before significant development can come forward.

Scrubs Lane Corridor

A mixed residential and commercial strip running parallel to the existing railway lines. Several schemes are already consented here, with construction starting on some plots. The proximity to Kensal Green (Overground and Canal) gives this area good existing transport links independent of HS2.

North Acton

The most mature part of the OPDC area in terms of residential development, with several towers already completed and occupied in recent years. The area has a developing identity as a younger, more urban quarter — popular with professionals working in Central London who value the Elizabeth line's speed into the centre.

What Residents Think

Conversations at Old Oak Neighbourhood Forum meetings reveal a community with mixed feelings. Long-standing residents welcome investment and improved amenities, but worry about displacement, the loss of community character, and whether the promised affordable homes will materialise. Newer residents — many attracted precisely by the regeneration story — are broadly positive, though frustrated by the slow pace of public realm improvements around active construction sites.

For more on what it's like to live in the area today and what the changes mean for daily life, see our Living in Old Oak guides. For the investment angle, read our property & housing market overview.

Timeline Expectations

The honest answer about timelines is that they have repeatedly shifted. The relationship between HS2's construction programme and the OPDC's housing delivery is tight — infrastructure delays ripple through into delayed development starts. The most realistic scenario for seeing the bulk of Old Oak North's homes delivered is the late 2030s, once the HS2 station is operational and the area's infrastructure is in place. Earlier phases, particularly in North Acton and Scrubs Lane, will continue to come through in the interim.
